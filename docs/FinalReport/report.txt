Design Overview: The Hands-Free Wheelchair Project

This document deconstructs the design of an innovative hands-free sports wheelchair, exploring the journey from a compelling human need to a sophisticated mechatronic solution. We will examine the key engineering and design choices that integrate advanced mechanical, electrical, and control systems to create a device that is both powerful and intuitive.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


1. The Problem & The Mission: Engineering for Accessibility

At the heart of any great engineering project is a compelling human need. This project began with a deep understanding of the challenges faced by wheelchair athletes.

1.1. The Challenge for Wheelchair Athletes

For wheelchair users, particularly in sports, a fundamental conflict exists: their hands are required for both propulsion and gameplay. This dual demand for steering, pushing, and simultaneously handling a ball creates a steep learning curve and limits performance.

Beyond the gameplay challenge, there is a significant physical cost. The repetitive strain placed on the shoulders leads to a high rate of overuse injuries. One study highlighted this disparity starkly, finding that 75.7% of wheelchair users who play sports experience rotator cuff tears, compared to just 36.3% of non-sport users. This physical toll is a major barrier to participation. The experience of athletes like Adrit Batra, who finds that arm fatigue and the difficulty of one-handed manipulation are constant hurdles in street hockey, makes this challenge clear.

1.2. Our Core Mission

The project's primary goal can be stated in a single sentence: To create a hands-free mobility device for wheelchair sports that prevents overuse injuries, reduces the learning curve, and makes recreational sports more accessible.

To ensure a manageable scope and a focused design process, the team concentrated specifically on the demands of wheelchair basketball.

1.3. Key Performance Goals

To guide the design, the team established a set of clear, measurable targets for the wheelchair's performance.

Engineering Performance Targets | Performance Metric | Target | | :--- | :--- | | Weight Capacity | Accommodate up to a 110kg user | | Max Linear Speed | Reach a top speed of 4.5 m/s | | Total Operating Time | Operate for a full 40-minute game | | Turning Speed | Complete a 180-degree turn in 1 second |

Meeting these aggressive targets for speed and turning required a drive system capable of unparalleled agility, while the 40-minute operating time presented a significant power management challenge—two key problems that shaped the entire design. With these clear goals defined, the team could begin to explore the engineering concepts required to achieve them.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


2. The Core Concept: How It Works

The solution is an elegant fusion of mechanical design and intelligent control, centered on an intuitive user experience.

2.1. The "Inverted Pendulum" Approach

The fundamental concept is similar to that of a Segway. The wheelchair operates as a self-balancing "inverted pendulum," where the user controls movement simply by leaning their body. This creates an intuitive, real-time control system that translates the user's natural shifts in balance directly into motion, freeing their hands entirely for gameplay.

2.2. The Three Pillars of Design

The project's complexity is managed by breaking it down into three core engineering domains that work in harmony:

* Mechanical System: The physical structure, drive mechanism, and safety features that form the wheelchair's foundation.
* Electrical System: The network of motors, sensors, batteries, and controllers that provide power and process information.
* Control System: The software "brain" that translates the user's lean into precise movement commands for the motors.

Let's explore how each of these pillars was engineered to bring the concept to life.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


3. Mechanical Design: Building the Foundation

Every advanced robot begins with a robust physical foundation. Here, we examine the mechanical design choices that give the wheelchair its structure, agility, and—most importantly—its safety.

3.1. The Sphere Drive System

The wheelchair’s agility is achieved through a unique drive mechanism: a large, central sphere propelled by three precisely placed omni-wheels. Omni-wheels are special wheels with small rollers around their circumference that allow them to slide sideways effortlessly while still providing forward grip. This configuration is the key to the wheelchair's freedom of movement, enabling it to:

* Translate: Move in any direction across a flat surface (forward, backward, sideways).
* Rotate: Spin on the spot.

This combination allows the user to mimic the fluid, 360-degree agility of an able-bodied athlete.

3.2. Solving the Grip Problem

A critical engineering challenge emerged from the materials used: the large sphere is made of polyethylene and the omni-wheel rollers are polyurethane. Both materials have low friction coefficients, creating a risk of the wheels slipping on the ball, which would make control unreliable.

The solution was both creative and effective: the sphere was coated with SBR (Styrene-Butadiene Rubber) sheeting cut into a pattern of hexagons and pentagons, exactly like a soccer ball. This high-friction coating ensures a reliable, no-slip grip between the omni-wheels and the sphere.

3.3. Key Structural and Safety Features

To ensure the wheelchair is both robust and safe, several key features were integrated into the design.

1. Adapted Chassis The core frame is adapted from a standard basketball wheelchair for familiarity and ergonomics. This is mounted on a rigid and modular chassis built from T-slot aluminum extrusions.
2. Transmission Simple and reliable spur gears are used to transfer power from the motors to the omni-wheels. This design is compact, efficient, and well-suited for the torque requirements.
3. Tilt Limiter A critical safety feature is the "Safety ring" built into the chassis. This passive mechanical stop serves one vital function: it limits the platform's maximum tilt angle to 15 degrees in any direction, physically preventing the user from leaning too far and tipping over.

With the physical structure defined, the next challenge is to breathe life into it. That requires an electrical system to act as the wheelchair's nervous system, delivering power and processing information.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


4. Electrical System: The Power and Brains

The electrical system is the central nervous system of the wheelchair, managing power distribution, sensing user input, and driving the motors. It's an ecosystem where every component has a critical role.

4.1. Key Components and Their Roles

A network of carefully selected components works together to execute control commands. Think of the Teensy as the "captain," giving orders; the ODrives as "engine room chiefs," regulating power; and the motors as the "propellers," creating motion.

Component	Function
Teensy 4.1	The central microcontroller, or "main brain," that runs the control logic.
ODrive S1	The powerful motor controllers that deliver precise current to the motors.
M8325 Motors	The high-torque brushless motors that drive the omni-wheels.
BNO086 IMU	The sensor that measures the user's lean and the chair's orientation.
LiPo Batteries	Provides the high-current, high-discharge power required by the performance motors.

4.2. Performance Reality: Operating Time

To achieve the 4.5 m/s speed essential for competitive sports, the design required powerful, high-torque motors. This decision directly impacted endurance, resulting in a calculated 7.5-minute runtime—a clear engineering trade-off that prioritized athletic performance over the initial 40-minute objective. This highlights a classic engineering challenge: balancing high performance (power and torque) against endurance (operating time).

4.3. A Robust Communication Network

To ensure the "brain" (Teensy) could communicate with the "muscles" (the three ODrive motor controllers) effectively, the team planned a crucial upgrade from a simple UART protocol to a CAN Bus network.

The primary benefit of CAN Bus is its ability to provide robust, high-speed, and reliable communication between the central controller and all three motor controllers, which can be connected in a simple "daisy-chain" configuration. This robust communication is a non-negotiable prerequisite for the LQR control system, which relies on near-instantaneous, coordinated commands to all three motors to maintain stability.

But powerful hardware is only effective with intelligent direction. We now turn to the control system—the software brain that translates the rider's intent into precise, stable motion.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


5. Control System: Making It Intuitive

The control system is the software intelligence that translates the electrical signals from the lean sensor into coordinated motor commands, creating a seamless and stable ride.

5.1. The "Smart" Controller: Why LQR?

The team chose a sophisticated control method known as a Linear-Quadratic Regulator (LQR). While the mathematics behind it are complex, the benefits for this project are clear and practical.

* It excels at managing complex, interconnected systems, simultaneously coordinating all three motors to control the chair's forward/backward, side-to-side, and rotational movements.
* It allows engineers to fine-tune the wheelchair's "feel" by adjusting parameters that balance aggressive, responsive movements against smooth, energy-efficient motion.
* Most importantly, it provides a mathematical guarantee of stability for the system. This is not just a performance benefit; it is a fundamental requirement for user safety.

5.2. A Safe and Efficient Design Process

Developing and testing such a complex control system was done using a modern, simulation-first workflow.

1. Modeling First, the physics of the wheelchair—its mass, dimensions, and motor properties—were translated into a precise mathematical model using MATLAB software.
2. Simulation Next, the LQR controller was developed and tested within a virtual simulation of the model. This allowed the team to safely test extreme maneuvers, push the system to its limits, and rapidly refine the controller's performance without risking any physical hardware.
3. Deployment Only after the controller was proven to be stable and effective in the virtual world was it deployed onto the physical wheelchair's Teensy microcontroller for real-world testing.

This methodical process leads directly to the project's current state and its path forward.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


6. Conclusion and Future Work

This project represents a sophisticated engineering effort to solve a real-world problem in accessible sports. Its design is defined by several critical, interlocking decisions.

6.1. Summary of Key Design Decisions

* Sphere-Based Drive: Chosen for its ability to provide unparalleled agility, allowing for movement and rotation in any direction, which is critical for sports.
* Lean-Based LQR Control: Implemented to create an intuitive and seamless user experience, translating a user's natural lean directly into motion while guaranteeing stability.
* CAN Bus Architecture: Selected to ensure robust, high-speed communication between all motor controllers, which is vital for the low-latency coordination required for dynamic balancing.

6.2. The Path Forward

The next steps for the project focus on fully implementing the advanced systems designed. The immediate goals are to integrate the IMU sensor for real-time state estimation and to deploy the complete closed-loop LQR control algorithm onto the Teensy 4.1 microcontroller, bringing this innovative hands-free wheelchair one step closer to the basketball court.
